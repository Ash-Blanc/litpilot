# Hackathon Mastery: Tips & Tricks from Winning YC, MIT, Stanford & More

**Author:** Reagan Hsu (@reagan_hsu)  
**Date:** March 1, 2026  
**Context:** 19 y/o Founding Engineer @browser_use  

I've won hackathon prizes at Y Combinator, MIT, Stanford, UCSD, and others. These are some tips and tricks I've learned along the way — and how winning a YC hackathon actually got me a founding engineering job at Browser Use.

## A Brief Snapshot

Almost all of the following advice is grounded in two things:

- Being **curious**
- Being someone who wants to **build things**

If you have both, you will inevitably be good at hackathons. Your ideas will wow others, your passion will shine through in demos, and your projects will be built from the perspective of someone building a real product — not someone just trying to win a competition.

## How to Get Ideas

Good ideas are hard to come by, and it's daunting to compete against others who have been thinking about a project for weeks (or months).  
But it's more than possible to come up with great ideas in a short time span.

### My top sources for ideas

1. **Talk to sponsors**  
   They know what projects are boring vs. innovative — and sometimes they'll straight-up tell you what they'd love to see built.  
   Sponsor products are often super cool and let you build things you never imagined — yet most people foolishly skip over them.  
   *Personal example:* I tried **Convex** at Y Combinator and haven't used any other database in a hackathon since.

2. **Start with a problem**  
   The most important aspect of a project is that it actually serves a purpose.

3. **Use personal ideas**  
   Good ideas are often personal ones (and vice versa). You already have:  
   - a valid pain point  
   - an idea of how to solve it  
   - a customer: **yourself**  
   This solves the two questions judges always ask:  
   - "Who would use this?"  
   - "Why was this made?"

4. **Don't force originality**  
   - Do **not** look at past hackathon projects for inspiration — it kills original thinking.  
   - Do **not** ask "How can I be different?" → this usually creates purposeless products.  
   Instead ask: **"What do I wish existed that currently doesn't?"**  
   If you can't answer, think harder — there are definitely many.

5. **Identify your inspiration channels**  
   Mine are simple:  
   - Writing random shower ideas in my notes app  
   - Saving cool-looking UIs on Twitter/Instagram  
   - While programming, always asking: "What would save me time, no matter how small?"  
   - Saving interesting technical concepts to apply later (recently: WebMCP)  
   - Keeping up with the latest Y Combinator startups

## How to Plan

1. Plan a **specific user flow**, keeping the demo in mind  
   Define the exact steps you'll show. Keep it short so judges can fully understand your product quickly.

2. Plan for a **visual wow factor**  
   Judges usually can't grasp deep technical complexity in a few minutes — especially with unfamiliar tools.  
   But **great-looking UI/UX** is universally appreciated.

3. Aim for **first place**  
   A lot of people optimize for sponsor tracks. I say: go for first place — especially with a strong team. Believe in yourself.

## How to Implement

- Separate **frontend** and **backend** early — link them later. Mock API endpoints on frontend if needed.
- Adopt an **"if it works, it works"** mentality. Hackathon projects are proofs of concept — make them work end-to-end, but no need to make code beautiful.
- Use **coding agents liberally** — I usually run ~5 in parallel. You should probably use even more when starting from scratch.  
  Dedicate some to planning, research, ideation, and implementation — all at once.
- If you mock anything, mock **non-core parts** (e.g. data feeds). Judges can easily tell mocked core features — and it hurts your score badly.
- Only add features that increase the **"wow" factor** without adding friction to the user flow.
- Reserve **at least 10 min (ideally 20 min)** to plan your demo. Submit early — you'd be surprised how long a tight 2-minute script takes.
- Keep your **main branch functional**. Build risky features on separate branches.

## How to Demo Your Project

Demos are the final (and often decisive) hurdle.  
Rule #1: Explain with as few words as possible — let the demo do the talking.

- Passion + concise confident speaking + good body language = required.
- Have **one short, sweet, clear sentence** as your opening pitch.
- Assume judges know basic full-stack principles and **nothing else**.  
  Avoid deep technical details unless you can explain them in ≤ 2 sentences.
- Use **slides** to aid explanation. Recommended structure:  
  1. One-sentence pitch  
  2. Problem  
  3. Solution  
  4. Demo  
  → Numbers / stats always help.

## Extra Tips

- If the hackathon is overnight → **pull the all-nighter**. Best ideas often come at 2 a.m. It's a canonical experience.
- Optimal team size: **2–3 people**. Four is too many; solo makes creativity & workload hard.  
  I usually split into frontend + backend; third person floats to help wherever needed.
- Be willing to **reject people** from joining — especially non-technical ones.  
  In my experience they rarely help with presentation or ideation (they can't explain technical concepts simply and don't know what's realistically buildable).
- Don't let time constraints stop you from trying something ambitious. You'd be surprised what you can finish in just a few hours (though < 3 is iffy).
- Good teammate traits I always look for:  
  - Willing to pull an all-nighter  
  - Well-spoken  
  - Technical

## Sanity Checks for Your Idea

Make sure you can confidently answer:

- Is it **showstopping**? Would any engineer see it and go "That's cool"?
- What **problem** does it solve?
- Who is it **for**?

## My Note to Builders

Hackathons can literally change your life.  
Winning the Y Combinator hackathon is what got me introduced to Browser Use — now I work here full-time.

Losing sucks — but it's usually very clear what you need to improve. That gives you a direct path to get better → and eventually start winning.

Most importantly: **each hackathon is like a day at a startup**. You are:

- Pushing coding agents to their limits
- Either revamping an existing product or creating something completely novel
- Learning to be a good teammate under high stress
- Working long hours with a high chance of losing in the end

In other words: **if you're good at hackathons, you'll be good at a startup**.

So what are you waiting for?
